The Day Everything Made Sense

June 15, 2023
Personal Diagnosis

I remember the moment so clearly. It was a Tuesday afternoon, unusually sunny for early October. I sat in a leather chair across from my new psychiatrist, a woman with kind eyes and silver-framed glasses who had been asking me questions for nearly an hour. Questions about my childhood, my sleep patterns, my relationships, and the periods of inexplicable energy followed by crushing depression that had marked so much of my twenties.

"Based on everything you've told me," she said, placing her notepad on the desk between us, "I believe you have bipolar disorder type II."

I expected to feel afraid, or sad, or even angry. Instead, I felt a profound sense of relief wash over me. Finally, there was a name for what I had been experiencing. There was a reason why I could feel on top of the world one week—productive, creative, barely needing sleep—and then find myself unable to get out of bed the next, convinced I was worthless and that everyone would be better off without me.

The Pieces Begin to Fit

As my psychiatrist explained more about bipolar disorder type II—characterized by hypomanic episodes (less severe than the full mania of type I) and depressive episodes—the puzzle pieces of my life began to click into place.

Those times in college when I would stay up for days working on art projects, feeling euphoric and full of ideas? Hypomania. The sudden decision to quit my stable job and start a business with almost no planning? Hypomania. The inexplicable periods when getting out of bed felt impossible and I would ignore calls and texts from friends for weeks? Depression.

For years, I had blamed these patterns on character flaws. I wasn't disciplined enough to maintain consistent productivity. I was too emotional, too flaky, too moody. Friends and family had offered well-meaning advice:

"Just try harder to stay positive."
"Everyone gets sad sometimes."
"You need to be more responsible."

But no amount of positive thinking or scheduling apps or meditation could fix the fundamental chemical imbalance in my brain. It wasn't a failure of willpower; it was an untreated medical condition.

The Relief of Understanding

Walking out of that office with my diagnosis, I felt an unexpected lightness. Not because having bipolar disorder is easy—it absolutely isn't—but because I finally had a framework to understand my experiences and access the treatment I needed.

It explained why antidepressants alone had made me feel worse in the past (they can trigger hypomania in people with bipolar disorder when not paired with a mood stabilizer). It explained why traditional advice for depression hadn't worked for me. It explained the cyclical nature of my moods that had confused me and everyone around me for so long.

That day marked the beginning of a new journey—one of medication trials, therapy, lifestyle adjustments, and learning to recognize my own patterns. It hasn't been straightforward or easy, but it has been clarifying.

Beyond the Diagnosis

In the months since my diagnosis, I've had to rethink many aspects of my life. I've had to educate myself about this condition and make difficult decisions about whom to tell and how much to share. I've had to learn how to monitor my moods, identify triggers, and recognize the early warning signs of both hypomania and depression.

But I've also discovered a community of people who understand exactly what I'm going through. I've found resources that have helped me navigate this new reality. And I've started to develop a more compassionate relationship with myself and my brain.

I no longer beat myself up for not being able to maintain the productivity levels of my hypomanic periods all the time. I no longer see my depressive episodes as personal failings but as symptoms that need to be managed. And while I still struggle with the unpredictability of this disorder, I'm learning to work with it rather than against it.

The day I received my diagnosis wasn't the end of anything—it was a beginning. A beginning of understanding, of proper treatment, and of a more authentic relationship with myself. It was the day everything finally made sense.
