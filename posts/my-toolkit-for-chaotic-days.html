My Toolkit for Chaotic Days

June 22, 2023 Coping Strategies

From medication routines to mindfulness practices, I'm building a personalized arsenal of strategies to navigate the unpredictable terrain of bipolar disorder. Here's what I've learned works for me.

Morning Routine: Setting the Tone

My day begins with a structured morning routine. It's not just about taking medication—it's about creating a foundation of stability. I keep a journal by my bedside, noting my mood and energy levels first thing. This simple act of self-awareness helps me prepare for what's ahead.

Mindfulness and Meditation

When my thoughts start racing or anxiety creeps in, I turn to mindfulness techniques. Sometimes it's as simple as focusing on my breath for five minutes. Other times, I use guided meditation apps that specifically address bipolar symptoms.

Physical Activity: Moving Through Emotions

Exercise has become a non-negotiable part of my routine. Whether it's a morning yoga session or an evening walk, physical activity helps regulate my mood and provides a healthy outlet for excess energy during elevated periods.

Sleep Hygiene

Sleep disruption can trigger episodes, so I've developed a strict sleep schedule. My bedroom is a sanctuary—cool, dark, and free from electronics. I start winding down an hour before bedtime with gentle stretching and herbal tea.

"The key isn't perfection—it's consistency. Small, daily habits build resilience over time."

Crisis Planning

I keep a written plan for both manic and depressive episodes. It includes emergency contacts, medication details, and specific steps for different scenarios. Having this plan reduces anxiety about potential crises and provides clear direction when I'm not thinking clearly.

Support System Check-Ins

Regular check-ins with my support system—family, friends, and healthcare providers—help me stay accountable and catch early warning signs of episodes. I've learned to be open about my needs and accept help when it's offered.

Building this toolkit has been a process of trial and error. What works during a depressive episode might not help during mania. The key is flexibility and self-compassion—understanding that some days require different tools than others.
